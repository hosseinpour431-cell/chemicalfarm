Hazard Classification

Corrosive; causes severe burns and eye damage

Acetic acid (especially glacial) is a corrosive liquid that can cause severe skin burns and eye damage. Vapors may irritate the respiratory tract. It is also combustible/flammable at elevated temperatures (flash point around 39–40°C).

Chronic Health Effects

Repeated exposure irritation

Long-term or repeated exposure to vapors/mists may contribute to chronic irritation of eyes and respiratory system. Repeated skin contact can cause dermatitis; prolonged exposure may erode tooth enamel in occupational settings.

Production Process & Capacity

Acetic acid is produced via two main routes: methanol carbonylation (dominant) and oxidation routes (secondary). Carbonylation is preferred due to high selectivity and scale.
1 Feedstock Preparation
Methanol is combined with carbon monoxide (CO) and promoter systems (iodide) in a controlled environment suitable for carbonylation chemistry.
2 Catalytic Reaction
Carbonylation occurs typically around 150–200°C and ~30–60 bar: CH₃OH + CO → CH₃COOH, using Rh (Monsanto) or Ir (Cativa) catalysts with iodide promoters; water management controls by-products.
3 Product Separation
Reactor effluent is flashed and distilled to separate acetic acid from light ends (e.g., methyl iodide, water, dissolved gases) with recycle loops to improve yield.
4 Purification & Storage
Final purification via dehydration and fractional distillation (as needed). Stored as glacial acetic acid or aqueous grades in compatible (corrosion-resistant) tanks with appropriate venting.

Global Demand Analysis

Asia-Pacific remains the largest consumption and production hub for acetic acid due to extensive VAM/PTA/PET value chains and large-scale integrated plants. North America and Europe follow with strong downstream demand in coatings, adhesives, solvents, and acetyls derivatives.
