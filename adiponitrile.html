Hazard Classification

Toxic; harmful by inhalation/skin; combustible liquid

Adiponitrile (ADN) is a key nitrile intermediate in the nylon 6,6 chain, primarily converted via hydrogenation to hexamethylenediamine (HMDA). ADN can be toxic if inhaled or absorbed through skin and should be handled with gloves, goggles, and effective ventilation. As a combustible organic liquid, avoid ignition sources and control vapor exposure, especially during transfer and heating.

Chronic Health Effects

Repeated exposure risk (systemic & respiratory effects)

Repeated exposure may contribute to ongoing respiratory irritation and systemic effects depending on dose and route. Favor closed handling, local exhaust ventilation, and strict PPE in sampling and maintenance. Implement occupational exposure monitoring where ADN is handled at scale.

Production Process & Capacity

Adiponitrile is produced mainly via butadiene hydrocyanation and purified for downstream hydrogenation to HMDA.
1 Feedstock & HCN Handling
Butadiene and hydrogen cyanide (HCN) are prepared under strict containment; high-integrity systems manage toxicity and prevent releases.
2 Catalytic Hydrocyanation
Hydrocyanation adds –CN groups to the C4 backbone to form nitrile intermediates, ultimately yielding adiponitrile; catalyst/selectivity control is critical for yield.
3 Separation & Purification
Distillation and finishing remove unreacted components and by-products; product is dried and brought to polymer-grade specifications for HMDA conversion.
4 Integration to HMDA
Adiponitrile is hydrogenated to hexamethylenediamine (HMDA), which is then reacted with adipic acid to produce nylon 6,6.

Adiponitrile is a strategic intermediate for nylon 6,6: ADN → HMDA via hydrogenation, then HMDA + adipic acid → polyamide 6,6. Safety is heavily driven by nitrile toxicity and (in upstream routes) HCN handling.
